# Zoroastrianism

## 1. Historical and Doctrinal Overview

Zoroastrianism, one of the world’s oldest monotheistic religions, originated in ancient Persia (modern-day Iran) around 1200–1000 BCE. It is based on the teachings of the prophet Zoroaster (Zarathustra), who taught that the supreme deity Ahura Mazda is in constant conflict with Angra Mainyu (Ahriman), the destructive spirit. This dualism of good versus evil forms the core of Zoroastrian ethics and cosmology.

The Avesta, Zoroastrianism's sacred scripture, contains hymns (Gathas) attributed to Zoroaster, along with later liturgical and legal texts. The religion was the state faith of the Achaemenid, Parthian, and Sasanian empires but declined after the Islamic conquest of Persia in the 7th century CE.

## 2. Original-Language vs. English Translation Discrepancies

The Gathas are written in Old Avestan, a highly archaic language. Translation challenges include abstract metaphysical terms with no direct English equivalent—e.g., *asha* (truth/order) and *druj* (falsehood/chaos). English renderings often soften the ethical intensity and cosmic scope embedded in the original poetic structure.

English translations may also obscure the communal or ritual aspects of Zoroastrian practice, emphasizing personal morality instead of ritual and cosmic harmony, which were vital to the original texts.

## 3. Oral vs. Written Tradition

Zoroastrianism preserved its teachings orally for centuries before they were written down. Much of the Avesta was lost over time, particularly during Alexander the Great’s invasion and later during Islamic expansion.

Oral tradition remains significant in the recitation of prayers and rituals by priests (Mobeds), particularly in the yasna and other fire ceremonies. The living oral practice is considered a vital conduit of divine energy, not merely a vehicle of preservation.

## 4. Treatment of Women, Minorities, and Non-Believers

Historically, Zoroastrianism afforded women a relatively high status in early Persia, especially compared to neighboring patriarchal societies. Female agency in ritual purity and household fire-keeping was essential. However, modern Zoroastrian communities (especially Parsi in India) have at times restricted women’s ritual participation and interfaith marriage rights.

Non-believers were traditionally regarded as spiritually neutral or misguided rather than evil. Zoroastrianism strongly discourages conversion, emphasizing inherited lineage rather than proselytization.

## 5. Core Ethical Teachings

The central ethical triad is “Good Thoughts, Good Words, Good Deeds.” Zoroastrian ethics is deeply action-oriented: each individual is seen as a cosmic agent in the fight against evil.

Fire, a symbol of divine truth and purity, is central not only ritually but morally—symbolizing the light of conscience. Ritual purity and environmental respect are also moral imperatives.

## 8. Belief System Complexity and Sub-Traditions

Zoroastrianism includes two major communities today: the **Parsis** in India and the **Iranian Zoroastrians**. Theologically, they are quite similar but differ in rites and openness to reform. The Parsi priesthood has resisted ordaining women or permitting intermarried children full rights, while some Iranian communities have moved toward inclusivity.

There are also apocalyptic and mystical Zoroastrian texts (e.g., Bundahishn, Denkard) that significantly shaped Jewish, Christian, and Islamic eschatology.

## 9. Views on Afterlife, Judgment, and Justice

Zoroastrians believe in a judgment at death based on moral balance. Souls cross the Chinvat Bridge—if righteous, they reach the House of Song; if not, they fall to the House of Lies. Eventually, all souls are purified during the final renovation (Frashokereti), when evil is destroyed, and the world is renewed.

This reflects a redemptive universalism that aligns with restorative justice ethics.

## 10. Stance Toward Animals, Environment, and Non-Human Life

Zoroastrianism has a deep ecological ethic. Water, earth, air, and fire are sacred. Pollution of natural elements is considered an ethical violation. Killing noxious creatures (e.g., scorpions) is permitted, but harming beneficial animals or degrading the environment is morally condemned.

This aligns strongly with **C6: Stewardship of Life** and environmental justice themes in the Accord.

## 11. Relation to Political Power and Coercion

Under ancient Persian rule, Zoroastrianism coexisted with other faiths, showing early forms of religious tolerance. However, during the Sasanian Empire, it was used to legitimize state authority and sometimes suppress dissenting sects.

Modern Zoroastrianism is largely apolitical and decentralized, with few institutional coercive mechanisms—generally aligning with Accord principles of non-coercion.

## 12. Summary Verdict on Accord Compatibility

Zoroastrianism is one of the strongest ancient alignments with the spirit of the Bronze Accord, especially in its core emphasis on moral agency, truth, and harmony with nature. Challenges include restrictive lineage rules and caste-like exclusions, but these are sociocultural rather than theological necessities.
